01

Sustainable Artificial Theatre Snow

Materials Science VDI 2221 Aerodynamics
Problem

Microplastic hazard in theatrical environments

Commercial stage snow relies on shredded EPS or LDPE — materials that generate respirable microplastics and pose severe inhalation hazards to actors engaged in high-intensity performance. No certified biodegradable alternative existed.

Engineering Approach

VDI 2221 Systematic Design — 7 Iterative Trials

The project followed the German VDI 2221 standard from abstract problem formulation through morphological box analysis to physical embodiment. Three material concepts were evaluated: Thermoplastic Starch (A), Cellulose Floc (B), and Alginate Aerogel (C). Concept B was rejected for borate toxicity; Concept C for prohibitive cost. Concept A was developed through 7 iterative prototyping cycles — progressing from direct heating (Trials 1–2) through double-boiler gelatinization, CO₂ blowing agent optimization, and mechanical constraint baking (Trial 7).

Key Engineering Insight

The crucial discovery was that aerodynamic flutter — not terminal velocity alone — creates visual realism. Measured velocity variance across 5 flakes (range: dart-down at 2.07 m/s to gentle drift) mathematically proves the chaotic multi-layered descent required for high-fidelity scenography.

The final Trial 7 matrix: High-amylose corn starch + Glycerin plasticizer + Sodium Bicarbonate blowing agent. During 90°C gelatinization, amylose chains hydrate and form a viscoelastic gel. Upon oven curing, endothermic NaHCO₃ decomposition releases CO₂ in-situ, nucleating micropores that reduce bulk density from native starch (>1400 kg/m³) to the target LDPE-equivalent range. Glycerin disrupts intermolecular hydrogen bonding, suppressing friability and achieving the flexible, planar geometry essential for aerodynamic flutter.

Particle density measured via gravimetric area determination: mass of irregular flake divided by (projected area × mean caliper thickness), verified across multiple samples.

02

Fault Diagnosis in HVAC Systems

Thermodynamics FDD Methods Industrial Survey
Problem

Undetected HVAC faults escalate energy consumption and operational failure

Modern HVAC systems comprising chillers, AHUs, valves, and dampers are prone to failures that — left undetected — increase energy draw, reduce occupant comfort, degrade equipment life, and can mask dangerous safety events like fires. Commercial buildings globally allocate up to 45% of total energy to air conditioning, making fault efficiency critical.

Engineering Approach

STM Model + Field Survey at GCORP Thane

Applied the Simple Thermodynamic Model (STM) for chiller Failure Detection and Diagnosis (FDD), monitoring operating parameters before and after maintenance events. Conducted a field survey at GCORP Thane (in partnership with Eco Vista MEP Solution Pvt. Ltd.) and quantified fault categories using a Pareto chart. Four distinct fault events were captured across a 12-month monitoring window.

Key Engineering Insight

The STM model's primary advantage is its simplicity: it requires only routine operating parameters (temperatures, pressures) to identify and localize faults — no complex sensor arrays needed. Root cause was condenser fouling in the water circuit, confirmed after cleaning restored thermal resistance parameters to baseline.

Material Selection: Copper piping selected over CPVC and mild steel for HVAC networks — lower reactivity (ranked with gold/platinum on the reactivity series), superior corrosion resistance across refrigerant contact cycles, and reduced refrigerant volume requirement through smaller allowable pipe diameters.

Key Components Assessed: 2-way chilled water control valves (flow regulation), balancing valves (hydraulic pressure equalization), Y-strainers (suspended particle removal), butterfly valves (flow shutoff), VAV boxes (zone-level temperature control via damper modulation).

Rocker-Bogie Terrain Robot

Mechanism Design Robotics Suspension Systems
Problem

Conventional suspension fails on rough terrain — planetary rovers need passive adaptability

Existing heavy-vehicle suspension systems using springs tip under asymmetric loading and require excessive gear reduction to overcome obstacles, critically limiting speed. The engineering challenge: traverse obstacles up to 2× wheel diameter while maintaining chassis stability without any springs, at the slow speeds (≤10 cm/s) needed to prevent dynamic shock damage.

Engineering Approach

NASA Rocker-Bogie Replication + Gear Steering Modification

Built upon NASA's favored planetary rover design (used in Sojourner, MER, Curiosity missions). The passive differential connecting both rockers to the chassis maintains the average pitch angle of both sides — when one rocker descends, the other rises, distributing payload across all 6 wheels at all times. A novel modification introduced a mechanical gear-type steering mechanism (master gear + 2 idlers + 2 wheel gears) operated by a single motor, replacing complex individual-wheel steering motors and reducing cost and design complexity.

Key Engineering Insight

The rocker-bogie system reduces body motion by 50% compared to conventional suspension by distributing load passively. The critical trade-off: the gear-type steering modification limits operation to relatively smooth terrain — a future iteration requires a more robust differential steering approach for rough-surface traversal.

Frame: Rocker arms fabricated from 5mm aluminum plate with punched lightening holes. Rocker-bogie joints use bearings for passive pivoting. Both sides connected via stainless steel rod-and-bearing differential. Main chassis PVC pipes joined with standard plumbing connectors (selected for cost and machinability).

Actuation: 6× DC gear motors (individual to each wheel) providing independent torque. Simulated rover mass: 260 kg equivalent (ATV-scale). Test track: 10 m² platform with cylindrical bump obstacle. Structural design inspired by JPL box-beam weldment approach — tapered thin-wall sections minimizing mass while handling combined bending and torsional loads.
